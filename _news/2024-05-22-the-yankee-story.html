In the days after US President Eisenhower signed the Atomic Energy Act of 1954, which allowed civilians to own and operate commercial nuclear plants, a group of 10 New England utilities agreed to form a cooperative company tasked with building and operating a full-scale nuclear plant. The company was called the Yankee Atomic Electric Company, and they built and operated the Yankee Rowe reactor.

1/9

The Yankee partners wanted to move fast, so they chose to use the same reactor model as the successful government-sponsored submarines and the under-construction Shippingport reactor: the Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR). They engaged the seasoned pros, Westinghouse and Stone & Webster to design and construct the reactor, the same exact team that was building Shippingport.

2/9

Yankee was somewhat unique (along with Dresden) at the time in that it was privately financed. The sponsoring companies purchased stock providing 35% of the capital needs. Another 35% came from the sale of Yankee bonds to insurance companies, and then the remaining 30% came from unsecured 4.75% notes from 28 banks. Here's the reactor structure going up.

3/9

Yankee also participated in the first round of the AEC's Power Demonstration Reactor Program (PDRP), which underwrote $5M of R&D and waived nuclear fuel usage fees. Here are the first steel plates being hung.

4/9

They used spherical steel containment shells back in the early days. Similar spheres were found on Big Rock Point and San Onofre 1.

5/9

Here's a detailed cutaway view showing the systems and components of the Yankee Rowe reactor. Click for great details!

6/9

Yankee went critical on August 19, 1960 and reached the full provisional license power of 120 MWe on January 17, 1961. It was delivered 23% under budget quickly began reliably supplying 14% of New England's electricity.

7/9

The early, on-budget delivery, private financing, relatively high-burnup core, and relatively stable performance of Yankee provided the proof needed to inspire the wave of orders of LWRs that made up the current nuclear fleet. Conversely, many of the non-water reactors being prototyped at the time suffered serious operational challenges.

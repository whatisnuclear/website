ENERGY

Context and issues

Energy gives us freedom

It is a substitute for labor and time

The formal definition of energy is "the ability to do work". In practice, if we can enlist machines powered by some form of energy, we can get what we need to survive and thrive without having to work so hard. Rather than farming all day every day, energy allows a small fraction of people to farm, allowing everyone else to farm only if they choose to. That freedom to choose is a luxury of a high-energy lifestyle.

Quality of life is correlated with energy use

Quality of life is hard to quantify, but the UN has attempted to do so with the Human Development Index (HDI), which combines life expectancy, education, and GDP. When you plot it vs. per capita electricity use, you see these two strong asymptotes. On the flat one you see the US and Norway. Here, we could reduce our energy usage a little bit without having a big impact on our lives. But on the low energy line, we can see that a tiny bit of extra energy is correlated with a massive improvement in quality of life. This correlation does not imply causation, but is very strong.

How a

NUCLEAR POWER PLANT

works

Eta Carinae (NASA/ESA)

In the beginning, one way or another, a bunch of energy was packed into heavy nuclei in great cataclysmic actions such as supernova and neutron star mergers. These made things like nuclear fuel atoms billions of years ago.

Uranium ore (Geomartin)

Now, we can go outside with a Geiger counter and wave it around and fine these same atoms that were forged so long ago in cosmic furnaces. They are sitting in our rocks and in the sea. (This is a good time to whip out a Geiger counter and uranium mineral if you have one).

Yellowcake (IAEA)

You then crush the rock and purify the uranium into this form, known as yellowcake.

Isotopic concentrations (whatisnuclear)

Uranium is found in nature with 2 isotopes. 99.3% of it is Uranium-238. The rest is Uranium-235 with fewer neutrons. They are chemically identical, but only U-235 readily splits and releases energy in a power plant.

Gas centrifuges (GAO-18-126)

Generally, we want to increase the fraction of U-235, and we do so by spinning it really fast so that the heavier atoms move preferentially to the outside. This process is called enrichment.

Nuclear fuel pellets (NRC)

Given enriched uranium, we then fabricate the fuel into ceramic pellets like these. Note that because uranium isotopes have very long half-lives (billions of years), it's perfectly safe to hold with a gloved hand.

Fuel assembly (NRC)

The pellets are then stacked into vertical cladding tubes and bundled together in fuel assemblies. This sets up spacing to allow coolant to flow between the fuel and pick up the heat.

Reactor core refueling (D.C. Cook)

The assemblies are assembled together into a core that is big enough to sustain a nuclear chain reaction. Every 18 or 24 months, nuclear plants shut down and open their lid to add new fuel and remove old. This is a picture of the D. C. Cook nuclear plant in Michigan refueling.

A neutron chain reaction

A neutron chain reaction occurs when one neutron fissions one nucleus, releasing the cosmic energy and also a few extra neutrons. These extra neutrons go on to split a neighboring nucleus, and the chain reaction continues. This allows us to access that slowly-bubbling-out energy from the ore much faster, enabling us to do useful things like make electricity.

A Pressurized Water Reactor (TVA)

We use a nuclear power plant to convert all the heat being released in the core into usable electricity. To do this, we just pump a coolant (typically water) through the core, where it heats up and generates steam. The steam blows through a turbine, like a giant pinwheel, which spins. The shaft is hooked to a generator that has magnets on it. The spinning magnets push electrons through coils which are attached to power lines and come to your house, where the moving electrons can be used in your appliances and lights.

Spent fuel pool (NRC)

After enough atoms have split, spent fuel assemblies are removed and placed into the spent fuel pool.

A dry cask (@ParisOrtizWines)

After ~5 years in the spent fuel pool, spent assemblies have cooled enough to be moved to air-cooled dry casks. They will sit in these outside the plant until the host nation establishes a national nuclear waste repository or engages in fuel recycling.

Swedish KBS-3 capsule for nuclear waste

In a repository scenario, the assemblies would be placed in storage casks, often lined with copper, and placed into a geologically stable area underground where they will remain until all the residual radiation has been emitted.

OTHER USES

of nuclear energy besides central power stations

Propulsion

Submarines and carriers (US Navy)

Merchant shipping (USG)

Space travel (LANL)

Nuclear reactors propel the US Navy fleet of submarines and aircraft carriers. This allows them to operate at high speed for vast distances without needing to refuel or use oxygen. Right after nuclear reactors powered submarines, they went on grand adventures like to the north pole under the ice and around the world submerged; things utterly impossible before nuclear power. We also ran a few merchant ships like the NS Savannah, allowing decarbonized shipping. We did build some prototype nuclear rockets as well, and NASA is looking into more.

District heat

District heat in Switzerland (Axpo)

China is making nuclear district heat (WNN)

When any heat source is converted to electricity, thermodynamics requires a certain amount of 'waste heat'. Rather than simply putting this heat in the air or water, reactors can be configured to pipe the warm water up to 100 km away to provide space heating in homes, greenhouses, ponds, and more. This is a fantastic way to decarbonize the fossil-heavy heating industry.

Nuclear waste solution options

  • On-site dry cask storage
  • Interim consolidated dry cask storage
  • Deep geologic repository
    • Mined repository
      • Rock (e.g. Onkalo, KBS-3, Yucca Mtn.)
      • Salt formations (e.g. WIPP)
    • Deep borehole
  • Recycling/reprocessing

Operational nuclear waste repository in salt at WIPP (DOE)

Finland's Onkalo repository (Posiva)

Nuclear waste disposal is already paid for by rate payers

  • In the US, the 1982 Nuclear Waste Policy Act set up a Nuclear Waste Fund
  • All commercial plants paid 1.0 mill per kWh generated
  • Current balance is about $55 billion
  • Payments were suspended in 2014 because repository stalled
  • Research and construction for Yucca Mountain spent about $10 B
  • Similar funds in Canada, Germany, Finland, etc.

A 2020 audit report of the Nuclear Waste fund (DOE)

Going forward on waste

  • Onkalo will clearly demonstrate the solution (if WIPP hasn't)

  • Need to find out how to get communities to reach out as repository host
  • Challenge anti-nuclear organizations who oppose solutions
    • Inconsistency: You can't simultaneously consider waste horrifying while stone-walling its safe disposal.
  • Alternate: recycling
    • Required for 1000+ year sustainability
    • Still needs repository for process losses and long-lived FPs
    • Far more expensive, and we need to make nuclear power cheaper
    • Historical proliferation issues ("plutonium!")
